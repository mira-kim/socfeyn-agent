In this chapter we continue our discussion of magnetic fields
associated with steady currents—the subject of magnetostatics. The
magnetic field is related to electric currents by our basic equations
\begin{gather}
\label{Eq:II:14:1}
\FLPdiv{\FLPB}=0,\\[1ex]
\label{Eq:II:14:2}
c^2\FLPcurl{\FLPB}=\frac{\FLPj}{\epsO}.
\end{gather}

We want now to solve these equations mathematically in a
 general way, that is, without requiring any special symmetry or
intuitive guessing. In electrostatics, we found that there was a
straightforward procedure for finding the field when the positions of
all electric charges are known: One simply works out the scalar
potential [eq] by taking an integral over the charges—as in
Eq. ( 4.25 ). Then if one wants the electric field, it is
obtained from the derivatives of [eq]. We will now show that there is
a corresponding procedure for finding the magnetic field [eq] if we
know the current density [eq] of all moving charges.

In electrostatics we saw that (because the curl of [eq] was always
zero) it was possible to represent [eq] as the gradient of a scalar
field [eq]. Now the curl of [eq] is not always zero, so it
is not possible, in general, to represent it as a gradient. However,
the divergence of [eq] is always zero, and this means that
we can always represent [eq] as the curl of another vector
field. For, as we saw in Section  2–7 , the divergence of
a curl is always zero. Thus we can always relate [eq] to a field we
will call [eq] by
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:3}
\FLPB=\FLPcurl{\FLPA}.
\end{equation}

Or, by writing out the components,
\begin{equation}
\begin{alignedat}{4}
&B_x&&=(\FLPcurl{\FLPA})_x&&=\ddp{A_z}{y}&&-\ddp{A_y}{z},\\[.75ex]
&B_y&&=(\FLPcurl{\FLPA})_y&&=\ddp{A_x}{z}&&-\ddp{A_z}{x},\\[.75ex]
&B_z&&=(\FLPcurl{\FLPA})_z&&=\ddp{A_y}{x}&&-\ddp{A_x}{y}.\\
\end{alignedat}
\label{Eq:II:14:4}
\end{equation}

Writing [eq] guarantees that Eq. ( 14.1 )
is satisfied, since, necessarily,
\begin{equation*}
\FLPdiv{\FLPB}=\FLPdiv{(\FLPcurl{\FLPA})}=0.
\end{equation*}

The field [eq] is called the vector potential .

You will remember that the scalar potential [eq] was not completely
specified by its definition. If we have found [eq] for some problem,
we can always find another potential [eq] that is equally good by
adding a constant:
\begin{equation*}
\phi'=\phi+C.
\end{equation*}

The new potential [eq] gives the same electric fields, since the
gradient [eq] is zero; [eq] and [eq] represent the same
physics.

Similarly, we can have different vector potentials [eq] which give
the same magnetic fields. Again, because [eq] is obtained
from [eq] by differentiation, adding a constant to [eq] doesn’t
change anything physical. But there is even more latitude
for [eq]. We can add to [eq] any field which is the gradient of some
scalar field, without changing the physics. We can show this as
follows. Suppose we have an [eq] that gives correctly the magnetic
field [eq] for some real situation, and ask in what circumstances
some other new vector potential [eq] will give the same 
field [eq] if substituted into ( 14.3 ). Then [eq]
and [eq] must have the same curl:
\begin{equation*}
\FLPB=\FLPcurl{\FLPA'}=\FLPcurl{\FLPA}.
\end{equation*}

Therefore
\begin{equation*}
\FLPcurl{\FLPA'}-\FLPcurl{\FLPA}=\FLPcurl{(\FLPA'-\FLPA)}=\FLPzero.
\end{equation*}

But if the curl of a vector is zero it must be the gradient of some
scalar field, say [eq], so [eq]. That means
that if [eq] is a satisfactory vector potential for a problem then,
for any [eq], at all,
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:5}
\FLPA'=\FLPA+\FLPgrad{\psi}
\end{equation}

will be an equally satisfactory vector potential, leading to the same
field [eq].

It is usually convenient to take some of the “latitude” out
of [eq] by arbitrarily placing some other condition on it (in much the
same way that we found it convenient—often—to choose to make the
potential [eq] zero at large distances). We can, for instance,
restrict [eq] by choosing arbitrarily what the divergence
of [eq] must be. We can always do that without affecting [eq]. This
is because although [eq] and [eq] have the same curl, and give
the same [eq], they do not need to have the same divergence. In
fact, [eq], and by a suitable
choice of [eq] we can make [eq] anything we wish.

What should we choose for [eq]? The choice should be made
to get the greatest mathematical convenience and will depend on the
problem we are doing. For magnetostatics , we will make the
simple choice
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:6}
\FLPdiv{\FLPA}=0.
\end{equation}

(Later, when we take up electrodynamics, we will change our choice.)

To get some experience with the vector potential, let’s look first at
what it is for a uniform magnetic field [eq]. Taking our [eq]-axis
in the direction of [eq], we must have
\begin{equation}
\begin{alignedat}{4}
&B_x&&=\ddp{A_z}{y}&&-\ddp{A_y}{z}&&=0,\\[1ex]
&B_y&&=\ddp{A_x}{z}&&-\ddp{A_z}{x}&&=0,\\[1ex]
&B_z&&=\ddp{A_y}{x}&&-\ddp{A_x}{y}&&=B_0.\\
\end{alignedat}
\label{Eq:II:14:7}
\end{equation}

By inspection, we see that one possible solution of these
equations is
\begin{equation*}
A_y=xB_0,\quad A_x=0,\quad A_z=0.
\end{equation*}

Or we could equally well take
\begin{equation*}
A_x=-yB_0,\quad A_y=0,\quad A_z=0.
\end{equation*}

Still another solution is a linear combination of the two:
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:8}
A_x=-\tfrac{1}{2}yB_0,\quad A_y=\tfrac{1}{2}xB_0,\quad A_z=0.
\end{equation}

It is clear that for any particular field [eq], the vector
potential [eq] is not unique; there are many possibilities.

The third solution, Eq. ( 14.8 ), has some interesting
properties. Since the [eq]-component is proportional to [eq] and the
[eq]-component is proportional to [eq], [eq] must be at right angles
to the vector from the [eq]-axis, which we will call [eq] (the
“prime” is to remind us that it is not the vector
displacement from the origin). Also, the magnitude of [eq] is
proportional to [eq] and, hence, to [eq]. So [eq] can
be simply written (for our uniform field) as
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:9}
\FLPA=\tfrac{1}{2}\FLPB_0\times\FLPr'.
\end{equation}

The vector potential [eq] has the magnitude [eq] and rotates
about the [eq]-axis as shown in Fig.  14–1 . If, for
example, the [eq] field is the axial field inside a solenoid, then
the vector potential circulates in the same sense as do the currents
of the solenoid.

The vector potential for a uniform field can be obtained in another
way. The circulation of [eq] on any closed loop [eq] can be
related to the surface integral of [eq] by Stokes’
theorem, Eq. ( 3.38 ):
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:10}
\oint_\Gamma\FLPA\cdot d\FLPs=
\underset{\text{inside [eq]}}{\int}
(\FLPcurl{\FLPA})\cdot\FLPn\,da.
\end{equation}

But the integral on the right is equal to the flux of [eq] through
the loop, so
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:11}
\oint_\Gamma\FLPA\cdot d\FLPs=
\underset{\text{inside [eq]}}{\int}
\FLPB\cdot\FLPn\,da.
\end{equation}

So the circulation of [eq] around any loop is equal to the
flux of [eq] through the loop. If we take a circular loop, of
radius [eq] in a plane perpendicular to a uniform field [eq], the
flux is just
\begin{equation*}

\end{equation*}

If we choose our origin on an axis of symmetry, so that we can
take [eq] as circumferential and a function only of [eq], the

\begin{equation*}

\end{equation*}

We get, as before,
\begin{equation*}

\end{equation*}

In the example we have just given, we have calculated the vector
potential from the magnetic field, which is opposite to what one
normally does. In complicated problems it is usually easier to solve
for the vector potential, and then determine the magnetic field from
it. We will now show how this can be done.

Since [eq] is determined by currents, so also is [eq]. We want
now to find [eq] in terms of the currents. We start with our basic
equation ( 14.2 ):
\begin{equation*}

\end{equation*}

which means, of course, that
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:12}

\end{equation}

This equation is for magnetostatics what the equation
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:13}
\FLPdiv{\FLPgrad{\phi}}=-\frac{\rho}{\epsO}
\end{equation}

was for electrostatics.

Our equation ( 14.12 ) for the vector potential looks even
more like that for [eq] if we rewrite [eq]
using the vector identity Eq. ( 2.58 ):
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:14}

\end{equation}

Since we have chosen to make [eq] (and now you see why),

\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:15}

\end{equation}

This vector equation means, of course, three equations:

\label{Eq:II:14:16}

\end{equation}
 
 

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}

\end{aligned}
\label{Eq:II:14:16}
\end{equation}

 


And each of these equations is mathematically identical to
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:17}

\end{equation}

Now we can raise an interesting question. Suppose we put a short piece
of wire [eq] perpendicular to the axis of the cylinder, extending from
the axis out to the surface, and fastened to the cylinder so that it
rotates with it, as in Fig.  14–5 . This wire is moving
in a magnetic field, so the [eq] forces will cause the
ends of the wire to be charged (they will charge up until the
[eq]-field from the charges just balances the [eq] force).
If the cylinder has a positive charge, the end of the wire at
the axis will have a negative charge. By measuring the charge on the
end of the wire, we could measure the speed of rotation of the
system. We would have an “angular-velocity meter”!

But are you wondering: “What if I put myself in the frame of
reference of the rotating cylinder? Then there is just a charged
cylinder at rest, and I know that the electrostatic equations say
there will be no electric fields inside, so there will be no
force pushing charges to the center. So something must be wrong.” But
there is nothing wrong. There is no “relativity of rotation.” A
rotating system is not an inertial frame, and the laws of
physics are different. We must be sure to use equations of
electromagnetism only with respect to inertial coordinate systems.

It would be nice if we could measure the absolute rotation of the
earth with such a charged cylinder, but unfortunately the effect is
much too small to observe even with the most delicate instruments now
available.

Let’s use the vector-potential method to find the magnetic field of a

dipole.” That is, it produces a magnetic field like the
electric field from an electric dipole.

Then we can write
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:34}
\FLPA=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsO c^2}\,\frac{\FLPmu\times\FLPR}{R^3}=
\frac{1}{4\pi\epsO c^2}\,\frac{\FLPmu\times\FLPe_R}{R^2}.
\end{equation}

We have still to find [eq]. Using ( 14.33 )
and ( 14.34 ), together with ( 14.4 ), we get
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:35}
B_x=-\ddp{}{z}\,\frac{\mu}{4\pi\epsO c^2}\,\frac{x}{R^3}=
\dotsm\frac{3xz}{R^5}
\end{equation}

(where by [eq] we mean [eq]),
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
B_y&=\ddp{}{z}\biggl(-\dotsm\frac{y}{R^3}\biggr)=\dotsm\frac{3yz}{R^5},\\[1ex]
B_z&=\ddp{}{x}\biggl(\dotsm\frac{x}{R^3}\biggr)-
\ddp{}{y}\biggl(-\dotsm\frac{y}{R^3}\biggr)\\[1ex]
&=-\dotsm\biggl(\frac{1}{R^3}-\frac{3z^2}{R^5}\biggr).
\end{aligned}
\label{Eq:II:14:36}
\end{equation}

The components of the [eq]-field behave exactly like those of the
[eq]-field for a dipole oriented along the [eq]-axis. (See Eqs.
( 6.14 ) and ( 6.15 ); also Fig.  6–4 .) That’s
why we call the loop a magnetic dipole. The word “dipole” is slightly
misleading when applied to a magnetic field because there are no magnetic
“poles” that correspond to electric charges. The magnetic “dipole field” is
not produced by two “charges,” but by an elementary current loop.

It is curious, though, that starting with completely different laws,
[eq] and [eq], we
can end up with the same kind of a field. Why should that be? It is
because the dipole fields appear only when we are far away from all
charges or currents. So through most of the relevant space the
equations for [eq] and [eq] are identical: both have zero
divergence and zero curl. So they give the same solutions. However,
the sources whose configuration we summarize by the dipole
moments are physically quite different—in one case, it’s a
circulating current; in the other, a pair of charges, one above and
one below the plane of the loop for the corresponding field.

We are often interested in the magnetic fields produced by circuits of
wire in which the diameter of the wire is very small compared with the
dimensions of the whole system. In such cases, we can simplify the
equations for the magnetic field. For a thin wire we can write our
volume element as
\begin{equation*}
dV=S\,ds
\end{equation*}

where [eq] is the cross-sectional area of the wire and [eq] is the
element of distance along the wire. In fact, since the vector [eq]
is in the same direction as [eq], as shown in Fig.  14–9 
(and we can assume that [eq] is constant across any given cross
section), we can write a vector equation:
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:37}
\FLPj\,dV=jS\,d\FLPs.
\end{equation}

But [eq] is just what we call the current [eq] in a wire, so our
integral for the vector potential ( 14.19 ) becomes
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:38}
\FLPA(1)=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsO c^2}\int\frac{I\,d\FLPs_2}{r_{12}}
\end{equation}

(see Fig.  14–10 ). (We assume that [eq] is the same
throughout the circuit. If there are several branches with different
currents, we should, of course, use the appropriate [eq] for each
branch.)

Again, we can find the fields from ( 14.38 ) either by
integrating directly or by solving the corresponding electrostatic
problems.

In studying electrostatics we found that the electric field of a known
charge distribution could be obtained directly with an integral,
Eq. ( 4.16 ):
\begin{equation*}
\FLPE(1)=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsO}\int
\frac{\rho(2)\FLPe_{12}\,dV_2}{r_{12}^2}.
\end{equation*}

As we have seen, it is usually more work to evaluate this
integral—there are really three integrals, one for each
component—than to do the integral for the potential and take its
gradient.

There is a similar integral which relates the magnetic field to the
currents. We already have an integral for [eq],
Eq. ( 14.19 ); we can get an integral for [eq] by taking
the curl of both sides:
 
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:39}
\FLPB(1)=\FLPcurl{\FLPA(1)}=
\FLPcurl{\biggl[\frac{1}{4\pi\epsO c^2}\!\int\!
\frac{\FLPj(2)\,dV_2}{r_{12}}\biggr]}\!.
\end{equation}
 
 

\begin{align}
\FLPB(1)&=\FLPcurl{\FLPA(1)}\notag\\
\label{Eq:II:14:39}
&=\FLPcurl{\biggl[\frac{1}{4\pi\epsO c^2}\!\int\!
\frac{\FLPj(2)\,dV_2}{r_{12}}\biggr]}\!.
\end{align}

 
Now we must be careful: The curl operator means taking the derivatives
of [eq], that is, it operates only on the
coordinates [eq]. We can move the [eq] operator inside the
integral sign if we remember that it operates only on variables with
the subscript [eq], which of course, appear only in
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:40}
r_{12}=[(x_1\!-\!x_2)^2\!+\!(y_1\!-\!y_2)^2\!+\!(z_1\!-\!z_2)^2]^{1/2}.
\end{equation}

We have, for the [eq]-component of [eq],
\begin{align}
B_x&=\ddp{A_z}{y_1}-\ddp{A_y}{z_1}\notag\\[1.5ex]
&=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsO c^2}\!\int\biggl[
j_z\ddp{}{y_1}\biggl(\!\frac{1}{r_{12}}\!\biggr)\!-\!
j_y\ddp{}{z_1}\biggl(\!\frac{1}{r_{12}}\!\biggr)
\!\biggr]dV_2\notag\\[2ex]
\label{Eq:II:14:41}
&=-\frac{1}{4\pi\epsO c^2}\!\int\biggl[
j_z\frac{y_1-y_2}{r_{12}^3}\!-\!j_y\frac{z_1-z_2}{r_{12}^3}
\biggr]dV_2.
\end{align}

The quantity in brackets is just the negative of the [eq]-component of
\begin{equation*}
\frac{\FLPj\times\FLPr_{12}}{r_{12}^3}=
\frac{\FLPj\times\FLPe_{12}}{r_{12}^2}
\end{equation*}

Corresponding results will be found for the other components, so we have
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:42}
\FLPB(1)=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsO c^2}\int
\frac{\FLPj(2)\times\FLPe_{12}}{r_{12}^2}\,dV_2.
\end{equation}

The integral gives [eq] directly in terms of the known
currents. The geometry involved is the same as that shown in
Fig.  14–2 .

If the currents exist only in circuits of small wires we can, as in
the last section, immediately do the integral across the wire,
replacing [eq] by [eq], where [eq] is an element of
length of the wire. Then, using the symbols in Fig.  14–10 ,
\begin{equation}
\label{Eq:II:14:43}
\FLPB(1)=-\frac{1}{4\pi\epsO c^2}\int
\frac{I\FLPe_{12}\times d\FLPs_2}{r_{12}^2}.
\end{equation}

(The minus sign appears because we have reversed the order of the
cross product.) This equation for [eq] is called the
 Biot-Savart law , after its
discoverers. It gives a formula for obtaining directly the magnetic
field produced by wires carrying currents.

You may wonder: “What is the advantage of the vector potential if we
can find [eq] directly with a vector integral? After all, [eq]
also involves three integrals!” Because of the cross product, the
integrals for [eq] are usually more complicated, as is evident from
Eq. ( 14.41 ). Also, since the integrals for [eq] are
like those of electrostatics, we may already know them. Finally, we
will see that in more advanced theoretical matters (in relativity, in
advanced formulations of the laws of mechanics, like the principle of
least action to be discussed later, and in quantum mechanics) the
vector potential plays an important role.
